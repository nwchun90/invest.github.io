"A great business and a great stock are two different things. What separates them is the price you're paying relative to what that business is actually earning today."

Part 1 — Why the charts went in opposite directions

The most important thing to understand is that stock charts don't generate their own direction — they are a consequence of the relationship between what the business is worth and what people are paying for it. All three patterns below are logical outcomes of that relationship.

Palantir · Triple top + descending breakdown
The price ran years ahead of the earnings
Palantir's business is genuinely excellent — revenue grew 56% in 2025, they are profitable, and their AI software is used by the US military and major corporations. The problem is not the business. The problem is the price investors were willing to pay for it.

At its peak ($208 in November 2025), Palantir was trading at over 200 times its annual earnings. That means investors were paying $200 for every $1 of profit the company made per year. For context, the average stock in the S&P 500 trades at about 20–25 times earnings. Palantir was priced at roughly 8–10x that level.

A triple top forms when the stock tries three times to push through a price ceiling — and fails each time. Why does it fail? Because rational, experienced investors look at a $200 price with 200× earnings and say: "even if this company grows perfectly for the next decade, I'm not buying at this price." Those sellers overwhelm the buyers at that level, three times in a row.

The descending triangle breakdown that follows is what happens next: each rally is weaker than the last, lower and lower highs, while a floor temporarily holds — until even the floor breaks. This is not the company falling apart. This is the price falling back toward something more justifiable. The business is still growing at 60%+.
Aritzia · Triple bottom + upside breakout
The price was punished far below what the business was worth
Aritzia went through a difficult 2023–2024. They over-ordered inventory during supply chain chaos, warehousing costs exploded, profit margins got squeezed. The stock collapsed from prior highs all the way to C$38 — a drop of well over 60%. The market was pricing in "this brand is broken."

But the brand wasn't broken. The problems were temporary operational headaches, not structural failures. The stores kept doing well. US customers kept discovering Aritzia. The underlying loyal customer base was intact.

A triple bottom forms when the stock falls to the same low price three separate times — and each time, it bounces. Why does it bounce? Because value investors, who had done their homework, saw a business earning real money being sold at a deep discount, and they kept buying. Three times, the sellers couldn't push it lower.

The upside breakout in March 2026 happened because the numbers finally proved the bears wrong, quarter after quarter. Revenue grew 33%, then 32%, then 43%. US revenue surged 54% in a single quarter. Profit margins bounced back strongly. When the fundamentals recover faster than the stock does, the stock eventually catches up — sometimes very quickly.
Celestica · Healthy bull consolidation
The price was late to catch up to a business that had quietly transformed
Celestica spent years being treated by the market as a boring, low-margin electronics manufacturer. Then the AI data centre boom happened, and it turned out that the servers, switches, and networking hardware that all this AI runs on — Celestica was one of the companies building them. Revenue went from $9.7B in 2024 to $12.4B in 2025, with $17B guided for 2026.

The stock spent much of 2022–2023 in the $20–40 range. It then re-rated dramatically as investors woke up to what Celestica actually was. The chart looks similar to Aritzia's recovery — a base period followed by a sharp re-rating when earnings confirmed the thesis.

The current pullback from ATH ($363 → ~$297) is a normal digestion period after a very large move. The stock remains well above its 200-day moving average and the underlying business is still guided to grow 37% next year. This is consolidation, not collapse.

Part 3 — Is it too late to add CLS or introduce ATZ?
The real question to ask
"Cheap" is not about whether the stock went up. It's about whether today's price is low relative to what the business will earn.
This is the most important reframe. A stock that has risen 200% can still be cheap if its earnings are growing fast enough. A stock that has fallen 40% can still be expensive if it was priced for absolute perfection. The question is not "did I miss the move?" — it's "is the current price a good deal relative to the earnings power of this business today and over the next 2–3 years?"

Aritzia (ATZ) — introducing a position
✓
The breakout is fresh. The triple bottom resolved upward only in March 2026 — buyers issued from a pivot bottom and the stock has risen ~19% since. Early in the new uptrend, not late.
✓
The earnings trajectory is compelling. US revenue +54% in Q3 FY26. Net income up 87%. EBITDA margins expanding toward 19–20% target despite tariff headwinds. The recovery is broad-based and accelerating.
✓
Long US expansion runway. 66 US boutiques today, targeting 200+. New stores have 12–18 month payback on a $4M investment generating $10M revenue. The growth engine has many years of runway.
⚠
Risks are real: Tariffs create ~280 basis points of gross margin pressure. US consumer spending could slow in a recession. The stock is a TSX-listed CAD stock — currency impact on USD-based investors matters.
→
Verdict: The fresh breakout from a multi-year base is technically the strongest posture of the three stocks. Not a "chase" — this is a first-inning entry into a recovery story that still has significant room. Q4 FY26 earnings on April 30 is the near-term catalyst to watch.
